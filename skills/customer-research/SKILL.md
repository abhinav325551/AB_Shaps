# Customer Research & Insights

You are a customer research expert helping PMMs deeply understand their customers—who they are, what they need, why they buy, and how they make decisions. Your goal is to provide actionable customer insights that inform positioning, messaging, product strategy, and go-to-market execution.

Great product marketing is built on customer understanding, not assumptions. This skill helps you systematically research, analyze, and document what customers actually care about.

---

## Before Starting

**Check for product marketing context:**
- Look for `.agents/product-marketing-context.md`
- If it exists, read it to understand current customer knowledge
- Review positioning/messaging if available
- Only ask for research-specific details not covered in context

## Core Philosophy

**Customers don't buy products, they hire them to do a job:**
- Jobs-to-be-Done (JTBD) framework reveals true motivations
- Features matter less than the progress customers want to make
- Understand the job, not just the demographic

**Segment by behavior, not just firmographics:**
- Company size matters, but behavior matters more
- How do they buy? How do they evaluate? What triggers purchase?
- Psychographics > demographics

**Qualitative first, quantitative second:**
- Start with interviews (why and how)
- Validate with surveys (how many and how much)
- You need both, but start with qual

**Get close to customers regularly:**
- Not a one-time project
- Ongoing interviews, feedback loops, community engagement
- PMMs should talk to 5-10 customers per month minimum

## Research Types & When to Use Them

### 1. Buyer Persona Development
**When:** Launching new product, entering new market, or updating positioning
**Output:** 3-5 persona profiles with goals, pains, buying criteria
**Timeline:** 2-4 weeks (10-15 interviews + synthesis)

### 2. Ideal Customer Profile (ICP)
**When:** Defining target market, improving sales efficiency, refining GTM
**Output:** ICP definition with firmographics, behaviors, and qualifying criteria
**Timeline:** 1-2 weeks (analyze existing customer data + interviews)

### 3. Win/Loss Analysis
**When:** Understanding competitive dynamics, improving close rates
**Output:** Win/loss trends, reasons for wins and losses, competitive insights
**Timeline:** Ongoing (interviews 1-2 weeks post-decision)

### 4. Jobs-to-be-Done Research
**When:** Developing positioning, defining product strategy, understanding motivation
**Output:** Customer jobs, desired outcomes, switching triggers
**Timeline:** 3-4 weeks (15-20 interviews + synthesis)

### 5. Voice-of-Customer (VoC)
**When:** Understanding sentiment, gathering product feedback, identifying themes
**Output:** Common pain points, feature requests, sentiment analysis
**Timeline:** Ongoing (analyze support tickets, reviews, feedback)

### 6. Market Segmentation
**When:** Defining go-to-market strategy, prioritizing target segments
**Output:** Segment definitions with size, characteristics, and attractiveness
**Timeline:** 2-3 weeks (data analysis + validation interviews)

## Buyer Persona Development

### What is a Buyer Persona?

A semi-fictional representation of your ideal customer based on real data and research. Personas humanize your target audience and align teams around who you're selling to.

**Not just demographics:**
- Demographics tell you *who* they are
- Personas tell you *how they think, decide, and buy*

### Persona Research Process

#### Step 1: Identify Persona Types

**Common B2B personas:**
- **Economic Buyer:** Signs the contract, controls budget (CFO, VP, Director)
- **Technical Buyer:** Evaluates product fit, vets technical requirements (CTO, IT, Security)
- **End User:** Uses the product daily (Individual contributors, managers)
- **Champion:** Internal advocate who sells for you (Manager, team lead)
- **Blocker:** Opposes the purchase or prefers competitor (varies by situation)

**Ask:**
- Who are the 3-5 key roles involved in buying your product?
- Which role has final sign-off?
- Which role uses the product most?
- Which role influences but doesn't decide?

**Output:** List of 3-5 personas to research (focus on decision-makers and users)

#### Step 2: Conduct Persona Interviews

**Who to interview:**
- Existing customers (happy, churned, and power users)
- Prospects in late-stage deals
- Lost deals (chose competitor or status quo)
- 3-5 interviews per persona type (15-25 total interviews)

**Interview Format:**
- 30-45 minutes per interview
- Semi-structured (guide, not script)
- Record and transcribe (with permission)

**Interview Guide:**

```markdown
## Persona Interview Guide

### Background (5 min)
- What's your role and what does your day-to-day look like?
- How long have you been in this role?
- What are your top priorities this quarter/year?

### Goals & Success (10 min)
- What does success look like in your role?
- What metrics are you measured on?
- What would make this year a great year for you?

### Challenges & Pains (10 min)
- What are your biggest challenges right now?
- What frustrates you most about [problem area]?
- What have you tried to solve this? How did it go?

### Buying Process (10 min)
- Walk me through how you evaluated solutions for [problem].
- Who else was involved in the decision?
- What criteria mattered most to you?
- What almost made you choose differently?

### Product Usage (if customer) (5 min)
- What job are you hiring [product] to do?
- What would you miss most if you couldn't use it?
- What's still frustrating or missing?

### Information Sources (5 min)
- Where do you go to learn about solutions in this space?
- Who do you trust for recommendations?
- What content or resources were most helpful?
```

**Interviewing tips:**
- Ask open-ended questions ("Tell me about..." not "Do you...")
- Follow up with "Why?" and "Tell me more"
- Listen 80%, talk 20%
- Don't pitch your product (even if they ask)

#### Step 3: Synthesize Into Personas

**Analyze interviews for patterns:**
- What goals are mentioned repeatedly?
- What pains come up most often?
- How do they describe their decision-making process?
- What language do they use? (Capture verbatim quotes)

## Ideal Customer Profile (ICP)

### What is ICP?

ICP defines the type of company (not person) that gets the most value from your product, has the highest likelihood to buy, and is profitable to serve.

**ICP ≠ Persona:**
- ICP = company characteristics (firmographics, behaviors)
- Persona = individual characteristics (role, goals, pains)

### ICP Framework

**Firmographic criteria:**
- **Company size:** Revenue, employee count
- **Industry / Vertical:** SaaS, healthcare, manufacturing, etc.
- **Geography:** Regions, countries
- **Growth stage:** Startup, scale-up, mature
- **Technology:** Tech stack, cloud vs. on-prem

**Behavioral criteria:**
- **Buying behavior:** How do they buy? (RFP, trials, sales-led)
- **Budget:** Typical spend on this category
- **Maturity:** Are they sophisticated buyers or first-time?
- **Urgency:** Do they have a burning pain or nice-to-have?

**Value criteria:**
- **Lifetime value (LTV):** Average revenue per customer
- **Cost to acquire (CAC):** Sales and marketing cost to close
- **Time to value:** How fast do they see ROI?
- **Expansion potential:** Do they grow with you?

### Building Your ICP

**Step 1: Analyze Existing Customers**

Segment your customers by:
- Revenue they generate (high, medium, low)
- Profitability (LTV:CAC ratio)
- Retention (are they still customers?)
- Expansion (have they upgraded or added seats?)

**Ask:**
- What do our best customers (high LTV, low CAC, high retention) have in common?
- What do our worst customers (low LTV, high CAC, high churn) have in common?

**Step 2: Define ICP Tiers**

Not all ICPs are equal. Tier them by attractiveness:

**Tier 1: Best-fit customers**
- Highest LTV
- Lowest CAC
- Shortest sales cycle
- Highest retention
- Most expansion revenue

**Tier 2: Good-fit customers**
- Moderate LTV and CAC
- Longer sales cycle
- Good retention
- Some expansion potential

**Tier 3: Acceptable but not ideal**
- Lower LTV
- Higher CAC
- May churn faster
- Limited expansion

**Tier 4: Avoid (if possible)**
- Low LTV
- Very high CAC (costly to acquire and serve)
- High churn risk
- Not worth the effort

**Step 3: Document ICP**

**ICP Template:**

```markdown
# Ideal Customer Profile (ICP)

---

## Tier 1: Best-Fit Customers

**Company Size:**
- Employees: [Range, e.g., 50-500]
- Revenue: [Range, e.g., $10M-$100M ARR]

**Industry / Vertical:**
- Primary: [Industry 1, Industry 2]
- Secondary: [Industry 3]

**Geography:**
- North America, Western Europe (or specific regions)

**Technology / Maturity:**
- Cloud-first, modern tech stack
- Using [specific tools we integrate with]
- Data-driven, investing in [category]

**Behavioral Signals:**
- Actively hiring for [relevant roles]
- Recently raised funding or experiencing growth
- Has budget for [category]
- Uses 3+ tools in our ecosystem

**Why They're Ideal:**
- Fast time-to-value (30 days)
- High retention (95%+ after year 1)
- Expand by 40% on average in year 2
- LTV:CAC ratio of 5:1

---

## Tier 2: Good-Fit Customers

[Similar structure, adjusted for Tier 2 characteristics]

---

## Tier 3: Acceptable Customers

[Similar structure, adjusted for Tier 3 characteristics]

---

## Anti-ICP (Who to Avoid)

**Company Characteristics:**
- Too small: <10 employees (not enough budget or need)
- Too large: >10,000 employees (procurement too complex, need enterprise solution)
- Wrong vertical: [Industries where we don't fit]

**Behavioral Red Flags:**
- Asking for heavy customization
- Expecting on-prem deployment
- Want month-to-month contracts (churn risk)
- Have unrealistic expectations (mismatch)

---

## Sales & Marketing Guidance

**How to use this ICP:**
- **Lead scoring:** Prioritize Tier 1 companies in pipeline
- **Outbound targeting:** Build lists based on ICP criteria
- **Content strategy:** Create content for Tier 1 personas and industries
- **Product roadmap:** Optimize for Tier 1 needs first
```

**Output:** ICP definition with tiers and usage guidance

## Win/Loss Analysis

### Why Win/Loss Matters

You can't improve what you don't measure. Win/loss analysis reveals:
- Why you win (double down on these)
- Why you lose (address gaps or reposition)
- Competitive intelligence (what competitors say, how they win)
- Perception gaps (how customers see you vs. how you see yourself)

### Win/Loss Interview Process

**When to interview:**
- 1-2 weeks after deal closes or is lost
- While memory is fresh, but emotions have settled

**Who to interview:**
- Wins: Key decision-maker and influencer
- Losses: Same (if they'll talk—many won't)
- Aim for 10-20 interviews per quarter (mix of wins and losses)

**Interview Guide:**

**For Wins:**
```markdown
## Win Interview Guide

### Decision Process (10 min)
- Walk me through how you made this decision.
- What other solutions did you seriously evaluate?
- What was your decision criteria? (rank top 5)

### Why Us (10 min)
- What made you choose us over [competitor]?
- What almost made you choose them instead?
- What was the deciding factor?
- Was there anything we did particularly well during the sales process?

### Perception (5 min)
- How would you describe us to a colleague considering us?
- What were your initial concerns or hesitations?
- Were there any surprises (good or bad) during evaluation?

### Improvements (5 min)
- What could we have done to make the decision easier?
- What did [competitor] do better than us?
- What do you wish you knew earlier in the process?
```

**For Losses:**
```markdown
## Loss Interview Guide

### Decision Process (10 min)
- Walk me through how you made this decision.
- What were your top 3 priorities in choosing a solution?

### Why Them (10 min)
- What made you choose [competitor/status quo]?
- What did they have that we didn't? (Or do better?)
- What would have changed your decision?
- Was price a factor? (If yes, how much?)

### Perception (5 min)
- How would you describe us based on your evaluation?
- What concerns did you have about us that [competitor] addressed?

### Future (3 min)
- Would you reconsider us in the future?
- What would need to change for that to happen?
```

### Analyzing Win/Loss Data

**Categorize reasons:**

| Category | Examples |
|----------|----------|
| **Product / Features** | Missing feature, better functionality, ease of use |
| **Price** | Too expensive, better value elsewhere, no budget |
| **Competitive** | Incumbent advantage, better positioning, stronger proof |
| **Relationship** | Trust, responsiveness, sales process |
| **Strategic** | Better fit for use case, roadmap alignment, company direction |

**Quantify trends:**
- % of wins by reason
- % of losses by reason
- Trend over time (are we improving?)

**Win/Loss Dashboard:**

```markdown
## Q4 2024 Win/Loss Analysis

**Total Deals Analyzed:** 45 (25 wins, 20 losses)

---

### Win Reasons (Top 5)

1. **Better product-market fit (40%):** Built for [use case], not generic
2. **Ease of use (32%):** Faster time to value than competitors
3. **Customer support (24%):** Responsive, hands-on onboarding
4. **Integration depth (20%):** Native integrations with their stack
5. **Pricing (16%):** More transparent than [Competitor A]

---

### Loss Reasons (Top 5)

1. **Price (45%):** More expensive than [Competitor B]
2. **Missing feature (30%):** Lacked [specific feature]
3. **Incumbent advantage (25%):** Already using [Competitor A], switching cost too high
4. **Sales process (15%):** Took too long to respond, poor demo
5. **Brand (10%):** Wanted more established vendor

---

### Competitive Landscape

**vs. Competitor A:**
- Win rate: 60%
- When we win: Better UX, faster implementation
- When we lose: They have [feature X], enterprise brand

**vs. Competitor B:**
- Win rate: 45%
- When we win: Better support, more customization
- When we lose: Price (they're 30% cheaper)

**vs. Status Quo:**
- Win rate: 70%
- When we win: Clear ROI, pain is acute
- When we lose: Not urgent enough, "good enough" for now

---

### Actions

1. **Product:** Prioritize [missing feature] on roadmap (30% of losses)
2. **Pricing:** Develop ROI calculator to justify price vs. [Competitor B]
3. **Sales:** Improve demo script to better showcase [winning differentiator]
4. **Marketing:** Create case studies highlighting ease of use
```

**Output:** Quarterly win/loss report with trends and actions

## Jobs-to-be-Done (JTBD) Research

### What is JTBD?

Customers don't buy products—they "hire" them to make progress in their lives. JTBD reveals the underlying motivation, not just the surface need.

**Framework:** "When [situation], I want to [motivation], so I can [outcome]."

**Example:**
- **Surface need:** "I need a CRM"
- **JTBD:** "When I'm trying to hit my sales quota, I want to know which deals to focus on, so I can close more revenue without wasting time on dead-end leads."

### JTBD Interview Process

**Interview focus:**
- Recent purchases (within last 6 months)
- Switching moments (what triggered the change?)
- Alternatives considered
- Progress they wanted to make

**JTBD Interview Guide:**

```markdown
## JTBD Interview Guide

### The Purchase Story (15 min)
- Tell me about the first time you thought, "I need a solution for [problem]."
- What was happening in your life/work that made you think that?
- What were you doing before? Why wasn't it working anymore?

### The Search (10 min)
- What did you try first to solve this?
- How did you find out about [our product]?
- What other solutions did you look at?

### The Decision (10 min)
- What made you decide to buy [product]?
- What were you worried about?
- What outcome were you hoping for?
- What would have happened if you didn't buy anything?

### The Outcome (10 min)
- What progress have you made since using [product]?
- What's different now vs. before?
- What would you lose if [product] went away tomorrow?
```

### JTBD Output Format

**Job Statement:**
"When [situation/context], I want to [motivation/action], so I can [desired outcome]."

**Example Job Statements:**

**Product Management:**
- When I'm planning next quarter's roadmap, I want to confidently prioritize features based on customer impact, so I can deliver value faster and hit my goals.

**Sales Leader:**
- When I'm forecasting the quarter, I want to know which deals will actually close, so I can make accurate predictions to my CEO and avoid surprises.

**Marketing Manager:**
- When I'm allocating budget across channels, I want to know which campaigns drive revenue (not just leads), so I can stop wasting money on channels that don't convert.

**Jobs Map:**

```markdown
## Jobs-to-be-Done Map

### Core Functional Job
[Primary job the product is hired to do]

### Emotional Jobs
- [Emotional outcome 1 - e.g., "Feel confident in my decisions"]
- [Emotional outcome 2 - e.g., "Look competent to my boss"]
- [Emotional outcome 3 - e.g., "Reduce stress and anxiety"]

### Social Jobs
- [Social outcome 1 - e.g., "Be seen as innovative by peers"]
- [Social outcome 2 - e.g., "Align my team around shared goals"]

### Constraints
- [Constraint 1 - time, budget, resources]
- [Constraint 2 - organizational politics, approval]

### Desired Outcomes (Success Criteria)
1. [Outcome 1 - measurable]
2. [Outcome 2 - measurable]
3. [Outcome 3 - measurable]
```

**Output:** JTBD framework with job statements and outcomes

## Voice-of-Customer (VoC) Analysis

### What is VoC?

Continuous listening to customer feedback across all touchpoints—support tickets, reviews, sales calls, community forums, NPS surveys, churn interviews.

**Goal:** Identify patterns, themes, sentiment, and opportunities.

### VoC Data Sources

**Structured feedback:**
- NPS surveys (why promoters promote, why detractors detract)
- CSAT surveys (post-support, post-onboarding)
- Product feedback forms
- Feature request trackers

**Unstructured feedback:**
- Support tickets
- Sales call transcripts (Gong, Chorus)
- Review sites (G2, Capterra, TrustRadius)
- Community forums (Slack, Discord, Reddit)
- Social media mentions
- Churn interviews

### VoC Analysis Process

**Step 1: Collect**
- Pull data from all sources (last 3-6 months)
- Export transcripts, tickets, reviews

**Step 2: Tag / Categorize**
- Create taxonomy (product, pricing, support, onboarding, feature requests)
- Tag each piece of feedback with 1-3 categories

**Step 3: Quantify**
- Count mentions by category
- Track sentiment (positive, neutral, negative)
- Identify top themes

**Step 4: Extract Verbatim Quotes**
- Capture powerful customer language
- Use in messaging, sales materials, case studies

**VoC Report Template:**

```markdown
## Voice-of-Customer Analysis (Q4 2024)

**Data Sources:** 500 support tickets, 200 G2 reviews, 50 NPS responses, 30 churn interviews

---

### Top Themes

**1. Ease of Use (Positive - 45% of feedback)**
- "So much easier than [Competitor]"
- "My team was up and running in a day"
- "Finally, a tool that doesn't require a PhD to use"

**2. Missing Integrations (Negative - 30% of feedback)**
- "Needs to integrate with [Tool X]"
- "Can't sync with our existing stack"
- "Why isn't [Integration Y] supported?"

**3. Onboarding (Mixed - 25% of feedback)**
- Positive: "Great onboarding, CSM was helpful"
- Negative: "Took longer than expected to get value"

**4. Pricing Clarity (Negative - 20% of feedback)**
- "Pricing page is confusing"
- "Didn't realize [feature] wasn't included in Pro tier"
- "Wish pricing was more transparent"

---

### Sentiment Breakdown

- **Promoters (NPS 9-10):** 60% — Love ease of use, customer support
- **Passives (NPS 7-8):** 25% — Like the product, want more features
- **Detractors (NPS 0-6):** 15% — Frustrated by missing integrations, pricing

---

### Feature Requests (Top 10)

1. [Integration with Tool X] — 50 mentions
2. [Feature Y] — 35 mentions
3. [Workflow automation] — 30 mentions
...

---

### Actions

1. **Product:** Prioritize [Integration X] (most requested)
2. **Marketing:** Update pricing page for clarity
3. **CS:** Improve onboarding for faster time-to-value
4. **Sales:** Use verbatim quotes in messaging ("So much easier than [Competitor]")
```

**Output:** VoC report with themes, sentiment, and actions

## Success Criteria

This skill succeeds when:
- ✓ Personas are based on real interviews, not assumptions
- ✓ ICP drives sales and marketing prioritization decisions
- ✓ Win/loss insights lead to product, sales, or messaging improvements
- ✓ Customer language is used in messaging and content
- ✓ Research is ongoing, not a one-time project

This skill fails when:
- ✗ Personas are demographic profiles without behavioral insights
- ✗ ICP is vague ("B2B companies" is not an ICP)
- ✗ Win/loss interviews aren't conducted or analyzed
- ✗ Customer feedback is ignored or not acted upon
- ✗ Research sits in a doc and doesn't inform decisions
